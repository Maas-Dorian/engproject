Our Anchor Text: "Chinks in My Armor"

Welcome to "Different shades of life." This project explores how our literacy is a reflection of our culture, our environment, and our personal journeys.

Our guide for this exploration is Julie Wan's essay "Chinks in My Armor." Wan describes building an "armor" to assimilate into American culture. However, "chinks" remain—eccentricities in speech or idioms that elude her, stemming from her "early Chinese socialization".

Wan reclaims these "chinks" not as failures, but as "intrinsic and meaningful" parts of her identity. Our authors explore their own "chinks" and literacy stories, connecting them back to Wan's central themes.

"My early Chinese socialization continues to frame and disrupt my literacy."

Connecting Our Stories

Bledion: Respecting the Story

Bledion's focus on telling "fair and respectful" multicultural stories (inspired by Patsy C. Robertson) directly addresses the negative experiences Julie Wan describes. Wan recounts being "linguistically marginalized and devalued" and how people made assumptions based on her appearance.

Bledion's reflection is the solution to Wan's problem. By advocating for stories that "avoid stereotypes" and "take time to understand others cultures," Bledion champions a literacy that respects the "multitudes of self-expression" Wan ultimately reclaims.

Chris: Literacy Sponsors

Chris's story of English not being his first language runs parallel to Julie Wan's. Wan describes her early English as "humiliating, isolating," and her primary "literacy sponsor" was her mother, her "de facto translator".

Chris's experience highlights a different path. His "literacy sponsors" were media, like Sesame Street. Both stories show that literacy is sponsored outside of school. This reinforces Wan's idea that our literacy is a "culmination of... singular life experiences".

Bryson: Environmental "Chinks"

Bryson's reflection on his rural environment and his use of "ain't" and "y'all" are his own "chinks in the armor." Wan notes the "eccentricities in my speech that I can't iron out" because they were "unheard-of in my ethnic household".

Bryson's rural dialect serves the same function—it's a marker of his environment that sets his language apart from "standardized English." This reinforces the core theme that our environment, whether rural China or rural Indiana, fundamentally frames our literacy.
